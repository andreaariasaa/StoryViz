Counting the Causes of Migration

By Andrea Arias, Jimin Lee, and Keili Tucker

To be a migrant is to have your life and your story politicized. Fleeing violence or natural disasters, seeking economic opportunities, or moving for a new job in a foreign country becomes a political act rather than a personal one. Over the past decade the number of migrants from Central America to the United States has been steadily increasing and unfortunately, prejudice against migrants and fears over “caravans” of migrants have been increasing alongside. But who are the migrants? What are their stories?



Our data comes from a survey of around 5000 households in Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador. Many of the respondents have family members who have migrated, considered migrating, or previously attempted to migrate, but many do not. For those who did consider or previously migrated, 83.4% of respondents said their primary motivation was to find better job opportunities. How does this relate to migration? Do these families want to migrate? Can they migrate?

Let’s investigate the first question. “Ideally, if they had the opportunity, would they like to move permanently or for a long period to another country?”


In the whole survey, this question was split roughly 50/50, though that is clearly not the case here. This may make sense given the story that is always told of migrants fleeing a lack of economic opportunity, though the reality is often that many people at this level of instability cannot afford migration. The average cost in USD for those who have attempted to migrate or have family members who have, was around $6000.
